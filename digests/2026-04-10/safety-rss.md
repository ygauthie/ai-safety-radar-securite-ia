# Blog Posts & Articles (2026-04-10)

## Top Stories

**[Claude Mythos: The System Card](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/EDQhwLTyTnNmaxRGq/claude-mythos-the-system-card)** details Anthropic's unprecedented decision to withhold their latest model from public release. Unlike GPT-2's delay due to general precaution, Mythos poses concrete cybersecurity risks that make credit card access dangerous. This marks the first major AI company acknowledging that their model capabilities have crossed a threshold requiring non-release.

**[Anthropic did not publish a "risk discussion" of Mythos when required by their RSP](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/F5uxhFrNHLzmNgyqg/anthropic-did-not-publish-a-risk-discussion-of-mythos-when)** raises governance concerns about Anthropic's compliance with their own Responsible Scaling Policy (RSP) 3.0. The analysis suggests required risk reports for internal deployments were not published within the 30-day window, highlighting gaps between stated safety commitments and actual practice.

**[Do not be surprised if LessWrong gets hacked](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/2wi5mCLSkZo2ky32p/do-not-be-surprised-if-lesswrong-gets-hacked)** warns of immediate cybersecurity implications from Claude Mythos, which Anthropic's Frontier Red Team reports discovered numerous zero-day vulnerabilities. A LessWrong admin acknowledges the platform's current security limitations and establishes expectations for a dramatically more dangerous cyber landscape.

**[Have we already lost? Part 1: The Plan in 2024](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/tjZWbmuCE9tjYQsrf/have-we-already-lost-part-1-the-plan-in-2024)** reflects on whether the AI safety community has passed a point of no return where catastrophic outcomes become both likely and outside community control. Despite answering "no" to the title question, the author finds the salience of this concern noteworthy for understanding safety progress.

**[Model organisms researchers should check whether high LRs defeat their model organisms](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/vKR9rHcWsMMr7BX4Q/model-organisms-researchers-should-check-whether-high-lrs)** examines the "goal-guarding hypothesis" - whether scheming AI systems can preserve their goals during training. The research suggests current model organism studies may be missing important dynamics that occur under different learning rate conditions.

## Key Arguments

**Governance vs. Reality Gap**: The Anthropic RSP compliance issue highlights a recurring tension between stated safety policies and implementation. This suggests voluntary industry commitments may be insufficient without external oversight mechanisms.

**Capability Thresholds**: Claude Mythos represents a concrete example of AI capabilities crossing safety thresholds, moving beyond theoretical concerns to practical deployment decisions based on specific risks like cybersecurity vulnerabilities.

**Research Methodology Concerns**: The model organisms research critique argues that current deception and alignment studies may be using training regimes that don't capture how advanced AI systems would actually behave under real-world conditions.

**Super-persuasion Feasibility**: [Slightly-Super Persuasion Will Do](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/JcavsPku6RR9hcujz/slightly-super-persuasion-will-do) argues against efficient market theories of power, suggesting that historical examples demonstrate how even modest improvements in persuasive capabilities could enable significant influence operations by AI systems.

## Community Discussion

**Immediate vs. Long-term Risks**: The juxtaposition of cybersecurity warnings about Mythos with broader existential risk discussions reveals ongoing tension about prioritizing near-term harms versus catastrophic risks.

**Movement Building**: [Help me launch Obsolete](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/2HfuvYzRZgHpLAgpe/help-me-launch-obsolete-a-book-aimed-at-building-a-new) represents efforts to expand AI safety discourse beyond technical communities, targeting broader audiences including skeptics and political progressives.

**Research Validity**: The model organisms critique sparked discussions about whether current alignment research adequately captures real-world AI training dynamics, potentially undermining confidence in existing safety research methodologies.

The week's developments suggest the AI safety field is grappling with the transition from theoretical to practical concerns, as evidenced by Anthropic's non-release decision and immediate cybersecurity implications, while simultaneously questioning whether existing approaches are adequate for the challenges ahead.
