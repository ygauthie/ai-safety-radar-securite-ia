# Blog Posts & Articles (2026-04-12)

## Top Stories

**[Catching illicit distributed training operations during an AI pause](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/35yyWJnXvC2ae6NKH/catching-illicit-distributed-training-operations-during-an)** - A detailed analysis of MIRI's Technical Governance Team proposal for an international AI pause, examining the technical challenges of monitoring distributed training operations that could circumvent compute thresholds. The post explores how bad actors might use geographically distributed clusters below the 16 H100 GPU registration threshold to conduct illicit training, and discusses detection mechanisms for such operations.

**[Pausing AI Is the Best Answer to Post-Alignment Problems](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/WtqD9pehq8p83cesT/pausing-ai-is-the-best-answer-to-post-alignment-problems)** - This post argues that even solving technical alignment leaves numerous "post-alignment problems" including misuse risks, AI welfare concerns, S-risks from conflict, gradual disempowerment, and concentration of power. The author contends that an AI development pause remains the most viable approach to addressing these complex governance and societal challenges before deploying ASI.

**[The Blast Radius Principle](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/rDzMraAEkBBewn7vy/the-blast-radius-principle)** - Drawing analogies from infrastructure warfare in Ukraine, this post introduces the "blast radius principle" as a framework for thinking about AI safety and resilience. The author argues for decentralization as a key strategy to limit the potential damage from concentrated AI systems, using examples of power grid attacks to illustrate vulnerabilities in centralized systems.

## Key Arguments

The governance discussion continues to evolve beyond pure technical alignment. The post on [post-alignment problems](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/WtqD9pehq8p83cesT/pausing-ai-is-the-best-answer-to-post-alignment-problems) presents a comprehensive taxonomy of risks that persist even with perfectly aligned AI systems, including misuse by malevolent actors, gradual concentration of power, and moral uncertainty around AI welfare. This represents a maturing of AI safety discourse to encompass broader sociotechnical challenges.

The technical governance space is grappling with enforcement mechanisms for proposed AI pauses. The [analysis of distributed training detection](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/35yyWJnXvC2ae6NKH/catching-illicit-distributed-training-operations-during-an) highlights sophisticated evasion strategies that could undermine compute-threshold-based governance regimes, including the use of geographically distributed clusters and novel communication protocols to hide training operations.

The [blast radius principle](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/rDzMraAEkBBewn7vy/the-blast-radius-principle) introduces infrastructure resilience thinking to AI safety, suggesting that decentralization strategies could be crucial for limiting catastrophic outcomes from AI system failures or misuse.

## Community Discussion

The LessWrong community appears to be deepening its focus on governance mechanisms and post-deployment challenges. The emphasis on AI pause proposals reflects ongoing debates about whether technical solutions alone are sufficient, or whether broader moratoriums are necessary to address the full spectrum of AI risks.

The discussion around distributed training detection suggests the community is taking seriously the implementation challenges of proposed governance frameworks, moving beyond high-level policy proposals to examine specific technical vulnerabilities and enforcement mechanisms.
